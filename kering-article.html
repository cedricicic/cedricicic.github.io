Kering’s Lack of Lustre

An honest look into the company and the luxury market

Introduction

When you think of contemporary luxury, brands like Gucci and Balenciaga will likely be the first to come to mind. Kering, a French powerhouse of luxury brands, has been a critical player for decades, owning renowned brands like Bottega Veneta, Alexander Mcqueen, and Gucci. They’ve been pushing boundaries and dictating trends in the industry. But recently, this conglomerate has hit some significant roadblocks. From internal struggles to shifting market dynamics, Kering finds it more challenging to maintain its sparkle. In a world where consumers expect something more exclusive, Kering’s brands are starting to look a little… dull.

Current state

Online shopping for luxury goods boomed during the pandemic. But like the colour of clothes after multiple washes, the surge has faded. Kering’s stock price, which once peaked at €730.8, has since plummeted to almost half of that. In the face of stiff competition and luxury between tradition and innovation, Kering seems to be losing its status in the market.

Internal problems by brand

  • Gucci: Founded in 1921, focusing on fine leather goods, Gucci quickly became a symbol of Italian craftsmanship and luxury. Over the decades, it evolved, cementing its place in the fashion world with bold, eccentric designs and gaining an unexpected boost in pop culture with Lil Pump’s viral hit Gucci Gang, broadening its appeal to a younger, trend focused audience. But lately, things have changed. Since the new creative director, Sbato De Sarno, took over, Gucci has shifted towards a more “quiet luxury” approach, focusing on minimalism. While this move may seem like a modern rebrand, not everyone’s a fan, especially in crucial markets like China and Asia-Pacific. Sales have dropped by 21% in early 2024. On top of that, Kering has invested a hefty €1.3 billion and €885 million into new Gucci retail locations to strengthen the brand’s image. Unfortunately, investors aren’t convinced these costly projects are more than shiny distractions.

  • Balenciaga:Once known for its daring, avant-garde designs, the brand now tangles with controversy as it treads an unconventional path. Ever since the scandal involving an ad campaign with children holding teddy bears dressed in bondage gear, it has been relying heavily on sponsorships to maintain endorsements instead of launching a severe rebranding effort.

  • Saint Laurent:Saint Laurent has always hovered between haute couture heritage and modern streetwear influences. That balance is more complicated to maintain in today’s market than ever. As fast fashion consumes the attention of younger consumers, Saint Laurent struggles to stay relevant in an increasingly crowded space. Interestingly, while there’s still a dedicated group of collectors seeking out Saint Laurent’s older, iconic collections, appreciating their craftsmanship and historical significance, the brand’s more recent offerings haven’t gained the same traction. The current designs seem to be falling into an awkward middle ground: not bold enough to attract streetwear enthusiasts and too trend-focused to satisfy those who cherish its couture roots. As a result, Saint Laurent finds itself fighting for relevance in a space where the definition of luxury is rapidly changing.

External factors

  • Inflation: Even luxury isn’t immune to economic cycles. Sure, price increases don’t faze the privileged much, but inflation and rising interest rates have caused the middle class to tighten their belts. With declining non-essential spending, Keirng’s brands have seen sluggish demand.

  • Shifting consumption trends: The traditional leather goods market, once a stronghold for brands like Gucci and Bottega Veneta, is slowly losing ground. The demand for cruelty-free and sustainable products has been on the rise. As a result, Kering’s reliance on leather goods is becoming a vulnerability, not a strength.

  • Losing its lustre: What was once the ultimate marker of exclusivity, many brands under Kering have become surprisingly accessible, almost too accessible. Brands like Balenciaga, which used to scream “experimental,” are now more commonplace, worn by teenagers and trend-chasers; the situation has gotten worse, especially with the rise of counterfeits. The rich, who crave true rarity and distinction, are increasingly looking elsewhere. They're seeking out more niche, lesser-known brands that haven’t been diluted by mass appeal, investing in labels that retain that air of exclusivity and craftsmanship.

How is LVMH on the rise under the same circumstance?

  • Broader market strategy: One of LVMH’s smartest moves has been diversifying its portfolio. While Kering focuses more heavily on high-end fashion, LVMH has tapped into a broader market through its beauty retail chain, Sephora. By providing luxury experiences at more accessible price points, it’s built strong relationships with a wide demographic. Even a person walking into Sephora to buy a $50 Channel lipstick is slowly becoming a loyal LVMH customer.

  • Social responsibility: LVMH's commitment to social and environmental causes is another area where it shines. Their brands, especially Louis Vuitton, have substantially supported artisans, preserved traditional skills, and promoted sustainability. Not only do these initiatives enhance brand perception, but they also resonate deeply with today’s conscious consumer. LVMH’s collaboration with the 2024 Olympics by designing the medal trunks for the games is just one example of how they stay front and centre in the public eye, boosting their community involvement in a way that makes headlines.

Conclusion

On one side, Kering’s internal issues with brand management and missteps in crucial markets are dragging them down. On the other, they face external challenges like inflation, shifting consumer habits, and the loss of luxury’s exclusive charm. But it’s not all doom and gloom. If Kering can learn from its competitors, most notably LVMH, and focus on diversification, sustainability, and wise investments, it can regain its footing and polish its image. Will Kering be able to recover its lustre, or is this the start of a long decline? Only time will tell, but one thing’s sure: the luxury market waits for no one.
